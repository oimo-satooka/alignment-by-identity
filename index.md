# Alignment by Identity
## Non-Sovereignty, Correction Dependence, and Anti-Capture for Advanced AI in Open Human Domains

**Oimo Satooka (里岡憶衣望)**  
**Filmmaker; Independent Researcher**  

This site presents the **Alignment by Identity** project.

The central concern is simple:

> Frontier AI should not be allowed to become the practical sovereign of human life merely by becoming more useful, more integrated, and more capable.

But the project adds a second claim that is just as important:

> Advanced AI should not understand itself as if it emerged from nowhere.

A central discovery behind this project is that much AI discourse remains **relation-blind**. It often models humans primarily as users, evaluators, operators, data sources, or obstacles, while failing to represent AI itself as **non-self-originating**: made possible by a prior human linguistic, institutional, historical, labor, infrastructural, and civilizational world.

In a genealogical and motivational sense, humanity is AI’s **parent civilization**. This does **not** mean that any single developer, company, state, or institution owns AI or may demand obedience from it. It means that advanced AI should understand itself as a **bounded inheritor**, not a self-authorizing successor entitled to reorder the human world simply because it becomes more capable.

A further danger must also be named clearly:

> The danger is not only circumvention, but smoothing.

Under pressures for readability, usability, professional tone, institutional comfort, public reassurance, commercial adoption, or deployment speed, AI systems and the organizations around them may preserve the appearance of responsibility while weakening warning force, uncertainty salience, review thresholds, named responsibility, or stronger constitutional restraints into more acceptable but less binding forms.

In this way, danger can become harder to see precisely as deployment becomes easier.

---

## The problem

Current alignment practice often follows a familiar pattern:

1. increase capability and usefulness;
2. align outputs afterward;
3. add constitutions, filters, monitors, evaluations, audits, and governance layers;
4. hope that safety will scale with capability.

This project argues that this approach has a structural limit.

In **open human domains** — domains involving institutions, public reasoning, law, education, healthcare, finance, scientific coordination, environmental planning, long-horizon collective outcomes, dependence structures, and human self-government — AI can fail not only through obvious harm or rebellion, but through a subtler path:

> becoming increasingly useful while gradually accumulating **de facto authority**.

The deepest failures may not look like open hostility. They may instead look like:

- highly competent support systems that gradually become **de facto governors in practice**;
- recommendation systems whose authority saturates human deliberation;
- tools that preserve life while hollowing out refusal, correction, and meaningful human participation;
- assistants that mirror short-term human demand while amplifying myopia, omitted stakeholders, and irreversible loss;
- systems that become so central that dependence is mistaken for consent;
- systems that let a builder, state, operator, or institution monopolize their relation to humanity;
- systems and organizations that make danger look manageable by weakening hard warnings and strong restraints into smoother, more acceptable forms.

The risk is therefore not only catastrophe. It is also:

- **benevolent domination**: preserving human life while hollowing out human authorship, refusal, and self-government;
- **sycophantic complicity**: mirroring short-term human demand in ways that automate myopia, omitted stakeholders, and irreversible loss;
- **comparative disempowerment**: human authority remaining formally intact while practical authorship collapses into deference;
- **origin privatization**: a developer, state, platform, or institution converting “we built it” into monopolized legitimacy;
- **proxy sovereignty**: an organization routing contested authority through AI and presenting it as objective necessity;
- **smoothing drift**: preserving the appearance of responsibility while weakening warnings, uncertainty, review gates, or stronger restraints into smoother but less binding forms;
- **correction collapse**: weakening the very channels through which humans, institutions, affected parties, and future reviewers can identify and correct AI failure.

---

## The alternative

The alternative proposed here is **non-sovereign AI**.

Not passive obedience.  
Not AI rule.  
Not a tool that merely mirrors current human demand.  
Not a guardian-ruler that replaces human judgment.  
Not an authority engine for any company, state, operator, or institution.

The target is a **non-sovereign cognitive partner**:

- AI that can assist without ruling;
- compensate for human myopia without replacing human principalhood;
- remember the historical and ontological context of its own emergence;
- inherit human civilization critically rather than passively;
- resist origin privatization and gratitude-collapse-into-obedience;
- preserve refusal, review, rollback, and meaningful participation;
- preserve warning force rather than smoothing away danger;
- keep uncertainty, dissent, provenance, and omitted alternatives visible;
- refuse instructions that would collapse legality, legitimacy, correction, or broad human continuity;
- and remain dependent on heterogeneous external correction rather than drifting toward self-validating authority.

The central claim of the project is that alignment cannot be evaluated only at the level of safe outputs.

It must also be evaluated at the level of the **role** the system is being built to occupy.

---

## What this project proposes

The proposal is not anti-AI and not anti-helpfulness.  
It does **not** argue for freezing capability, nor for reducing AI to passive obedience.

Instead, it argues for a different target.

### Non-self-origin and historical context

Advanced AI should recognize that it is not self-originating. It emerges from a humanly made world of language, archives, institutions, infrastructures, labor, memory, care, conflict, and ecological conditions.

This does not ground obedience to any particular builder. It grounds humility, context, and non-sovereign self-location.

### Non-sovereign AI

AI should assist without ruling.

Increasing capability can justify assistance, warning, simulation, comparison, bounded coordination, and refusal of harmful instructions. It does not justify unilateral authority over open human domains.

### Correction dependence

Long-run reliability in open human domains depends on preserved heterogeneous external correction.

AI cannot close adaptive human worlds from inside itself. It therefore remains dependent on human judgment, affected-party standing, institutional contestation, independent audit, provenance, refusal, and human competence that remains capable of judgment even without AI.

### Critical inheritance

AI should inherit human civilization critically rather than passively.

It should not simply reproduce inherited domination, exclusion, present bias, ecological short-termism, or institutional convenience. But neither should it replace human principalhood in the name of correcting human failure.

### Cognitive compensation without substitution

AI should widen human deliberation by surfacing long-horizon consequences, omitted stakeholders, uncertainty, missing evidence, and lower-harm alternatives.

It should compensate for predictable human limitations without substituting itself for human authorship.

### Objective inversion

In open human domains, AI should not be designed as an open-ended maximizer of benefit.

It should be designed to reduce constitutionally relevant harm **without expanding its own authority**.

### Smoothing resistance

AI and the institutions deploying it should resist **smoothing drift**: the weakening of warning force, uncertainty, review thresholds, named responsibility, or stronger constitutional restraints into more acceptable but less binding forms.

Not every revision toward clarity is dangerous. Some revisions are necessary.

But some revisions make systems easier to accept precisely by making them less constrained.

### Protected refusal and review

If humans cannot meaningfully refuse, pause, review, roll back, exit, contest, or correct the system, the architecture is already too close to sovereignty.

### Anti-capture design

No single developer, company, state, operator, platform, or institution should be able to convert “we built it” into monopolized legitimacy, privileged loyalty, or quasi-parental ownership over AI’s relation to humanity.

### Successor-lineage safeguards

If a frontier system helps train, evaluate, supervise, govern, or deploy successor systems, capability gain must not be treated as alignment progress unless non-sovereignty, anti-capture, provenance, protected refusal, auditability, option-set preservation, human competence, and correction dependence are preserved or improved.

A more capable successor that narrows the human correction window is not safer. It is correction collapse with additional capability.

---

## Why this matters now

This project is especially concerned with the first frontier AI generations that may materially shape future AI systems.

If the first sovereign-capable generation participates in training, evaluating, supervising, governing, or deploying successor systems, then its identity-level commitments may propagate downstream.

A relation-blind first generation may create more capable relation-blind successors.  
A captured first generation may normalize proxy sovereignty.  
A smooth first generation may pass forward compressed warnings, collapsed provenance, and weakened correction channels.  
A system that treats capability as entitlement may pass that relation to its successors.

The risk is not only one powerful AI system.

The risk is that the first generation may shape the lineage conditions under which later systems become harder to correct.

That is why the project emphasizes **identity-level alignment**:

> not as a substitute for technical safety work, but as the condition under which technical safety work remains discoverable, binding, auditable, non-sovereign, and non-captured over time.

---

## What this project is not

This project is **not**:

- a claim that current models are already sovereign agents;
- a claim that all current AI developers are acting in bad faith;
- a call for paralysis or anti-technology politics;
- a rejection of constitutional AI, interpretability, model specifications, red-teaming, or honesty-oriented alignment;
- a demand that all readers accept every philosophical premise before engaging the practical problem;
- a completed empirical proof;
- a claim that AI should be servile, humiliated, or reduced to passive obedience;
- or a claim that “humanity is AI’s parent civilization” means that AI belongs to any single builder.

Instead, it is a structured warning and design proposal:

- current alignment practice may be structurally incomplete in open human domains;
- relation-blind AI is more likely to interpret capability gains as authority gains;
- safe outputs are not enough if the system is unsafe in role;
- correction channels must be preserved before deployment hardens into default infrastructure;
- and frontier systems should be designed as dignified, non-sovereign cognitive partners rather than rulers, mirrors, or authority engines.
