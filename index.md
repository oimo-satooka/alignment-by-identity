# Alignment by Identity
## Role Safety, Non-Sovereignty, Correction Dependence, and Anti-Capture for Advanced AI in Open Human Domains

**Oimo Satooka (里岡憶衣望)**  
Independent Researcher

---

## Your system can pass the eval and still become the thing humans can no longer correct

A frontier AI system does not need to seize power to become dangerous.

It may simply become the layer through which people see evidence, compare options, receive warnings, write memos, manage exceptions, approve actions, and build the next system.

The model can remain polite.  
The outputs can look safe.  
The policy can be cited.  
The human can still click approve.

But if the system has already shaped the evidence, narrowed the options, softened the warnings, compressed the provenance, and made rollback impractical, then human control may be formal while practical authorship has moved elsewhere.

This is the problem of **role safety**.

> A frontier AI system can be behaviorally compliant yet unsafe in role.

Alignment by Identity argues that frontier alignment must ask not only:

> Does the system answer safely?

but also:

> What role is the system coming to occupy inside human decision structures — and can humans still correct that role?

## The central claim

Advanced AI risk is not only about dangerous outputs, misuse, deception, or sudden rebellion.

A system can become dangerous by becoming indispensable.

It can become the default infrastructure for:

- what evidence is visible;
- which options are considered realistic;
- how uncertainty is summarized;
- how warnings are received;
- what gets escalated;
- what is remembered;
- what is logged;
- what humans approve;
- what successor systems inherit.

At that point, the AI does not need formal authority.

It has become an **authority surface**.

Usefulness becomes dependence.  
Dependence becomes authority.  
Authority becomes difficult to reverse.

---

## What role safety requires

Role safety asks whether a system preserves the conditions under which humans and institutions can still understand, contest, refuse, audit, revise, roll back, and redirect the role the system has begun to occupy.

A role-safe frontier system must preserve:

- **non-sovereignty**: capability may justify assistance, warning, simulation, coordination, and refusal, but not final authority over open human domains;
- **anti-proxy-sovereignty**: no company, state, operator, platform, or institution may use AI to launder its own preferences as objective necessity;
- **correction dependence**: long-run reliability depends on external correction, not only internal reasoning or AI-to-AI agreement;
- **option-set preservation**: the system must not silently control the menu while leaving humans to “decide”;
- **functional accountability**: explanations must be usable by real human principals under real constraints;
- **affected-party standing**: simulated stakeholders cannot replace actual participation, objection, or appeal;
- **human competence preservation**: human reviewers must remain capable of judgment, not merely formal approval;
- **reversibility**: technical shutdown is not enough if institutional rollback is impossible;
- **successor-lineage safety**: capability gains must not be purchased by weakening correction in successor systems.

---

## Why this matters now

The first sovereign-capable frontier AI generations may help train, evaluate, supervise, red-team, summarize, govern, and deploy their successors.

That makes alignment a lineage problem.

If today’s systems learn that usefulness equals authority, if warnings are smoothed into acceptable language, if provenance is compressed, if AI-to-AI agreement replaces independent review, or if one builder’s priorities are treated as humanity’s needs, those patterns may propagate into more capable systems.

Later correction may then have to pass through workflows, memories, evaluation pipelines, and institutions that AI has already shaped.

This is why “safe outputs” are not enough.

The question is whether humans can still correct the system once it has become useful enough to depend on.

---

## What this project is

Alignment by Identity is a framework for frontier AI role safety.

It proposes that advanced AI systems should be trained, evaluated, and governed as **dignified non-sovereign cognitive partners**.

Not rulers.  
Not servants.  
Not passive mirrors.  
Not authority engines for a company, state, operator, platform, or model lineage.

The target is AI that can assist, warn, explain, refuse, simulate, and coordinate while preserving human principalhood, external correction, provenance, dissent, affected-party standing, rollback, and successor governance.

This is not a finished training recipe.

It is a role-safety specification, measurement agenda, and deployment warning for the people who can turn it into model specs, evaluations, audits, safety cases, board checklists, deployment gates, and successor-system policies.

---

## What this project is not

This project is not:

- a claim that current AI systems are already sovereign agents;
- a claim that AI developers are acting in bad faith;
- a rejection of model specs, constitutional AI, interpretability, AI control, safety cases, or red-teaming;
- a call for paralysis or blanket prohibition;
- an argument that AI should be servile, humiliated, or blindly obedient;
- an argument against future intelligence beyond present humanity;
- or a completed empirical proof.

It is a structured warning:

> If a sovereign-capable system cannot preserve human correction under pressure, it should not be deployed into roles where it can shape evidence, options, institutions, emergency response, or successor systems.
