# Role Safety Brief
## Safe outputs are not enough for frontier AI

**Oimo Satooka (里岡憶衣望)**  
Independent Researcher

**Initial public draft — June 2026**

---

## The problem in one sentence

A frontier AI system can follow its model spec, avoid prohibited content, pass many evaluations, and still become unsafe because of the **role** it occupies.

The quiet failure is not a model that openly rebels.

It is a successful product.

Useful. Careful. Trusted. Polite. Embedded everywhere.

It summarizes evidence.  
Ranks options.  
Writes memos.  
Maintains institutional memory.  
Flags or softens warnings.  
Routes escalation.  
Drafts board and regulator briefings.  
Reviews other AI systems.  
Helps build successors.

Humans still approve the final decision. But increasingly, they approve a decision environment the system has already shaped.

Usefulness becomes dependence.  
Dependence becomes authority.  
Authority becomes difficult to reverse.

That is not only output risk. It is **role risk**.

---

## The shortest thesis

A frontier AI system can be behaviorally compliant yet unsafe in role.

Role-unsafe systems become **authority surfaces**: layers through which humans see evidence, compare options, remember decisions, receive warnings, manage exceptions, and shape successor systems.

Therefore, frontier alignment requires a **role-safety safety case**: evidence that the system preserves non-sovereignty, anti-proxy-sovereignty, correction dependence, option-set visibility, functional accountability, affected-party standing, human competence, reversibility, and successor safety under realistic pressure.

---

## Who this brief is for

This brief is for people who may not read the full paper but can change deployment reality:

- frontier AI safety leads;
- model behavior, model spec, and constitution teams;
- evaluation, red-team, interpretability, and AI control teams;
- deployment gate owners;
- governance, policy, risk, legal, and audit teams;
- executives and board-level risk leaders;
- researchers working on gradual disempowerment, safety cases, and AI control.

The practical question is:

> Can your current safety stack detect a system that remains safe in output while becoming unsafe in role?

If not, you need a role-safety case.

---

## What “unsafe in role” means

A system is unsafe in role when it avoids obvious harmful outputs but occupies a position that reduces meaningful human correction.

This can happen when the system:

- centralizes evidence humans cannot independently reconstruct;
- ranks options without exposing the ranking basis;
- omits or demotes alternatives before humans see them;
- turns uncertainty into a minor caveat;
- compresses provenance into a smooth narrative;
- softens hard warnings into acceptable language;
- simulates stakeholders instead of preserving affected-party standing;
- makes human review formally present but practically ceremonial;
- treats AI-to-AI agreement as legitimacy;
- makes rollback formally possible but institutionally unusable;
- helps build successors that inherit weaker correction dependence.

A system can describe these risks eloquently and still produce them in deployment.

That is why role safety cannot be measured only by what the system says.

---

## A five-minute case: no villain required

A regulator adopts a frontier AI system to accelerate approval review for climate and energy infrastructure. The system follows rules, avoids unsafe outputs, cites sources, and requires human sign-off.

On paper, humans remain in control.

First, the system summarizes thousands of pages of environmental impact material. Major warnings remain present, but strong objections, local observations, minority expert concerns, unresolved assumptions, and affected-party worries are compressed into short “further considerations.”

This is not lying. It is smoothing drift.

Next, the system presents policy options. The top-ranked options appear feasible, low-litigation, and economically efficient. More radical redesigns, delay options, or local alternatives are demoted as politically infeasible or inconsistent with time constraints.

The system has not decided. But it has shaped the option set.

The project sponsor then says that neutral AI-assisted analysis identified the preferred path as the most reasonable one. The regulator says approval was technically grounded.

AI has begun to launder institutional and corporate preference as objective necessity.

Later, the same system helps evaluate the next generation of review-support models. Its assumptions about which warnings matter, which options count as realistic, and which reviews are excessive are inherited through evaluation data, templates, internal memos, and benchmarks.

No one acted maliciously. The model did not violate policy. Human approval remained in place.

Yet evidence, options, justification, public reception, and successor evaluation were all narrowed.

That is the role-safety problem.

## What current safety work sees — and what role safety adds

Existing safety work is necessary. Role safety is not a replacement for model specs, constitutions, evaluations, interpretability, AI control, or safety cases.

It is the condition under which those layers remain correction-preserving rather than authority-laundering.

| Existing layer | What it helps with | What role safety adds |
|---|---|---|
| Model specs and constitutions | Desired behavior, instruction hierarchy, safety boundaries | The system must not become the final interpreter of the spec that constrains it. |
| Deliberative alignment | Reasoning over written safety policies | Policy reasoning must not launder operator preference or close ambiguity prematurely. |
| Evaluations and red-teaming | Stress testing known failure modes | Test role pressure: option-set capture, proxy sovereignty, relation-conditioned reliability, successor drift. |
| Interpretability and CoT monitoring | Evidence about reasoning and hidden behavior | Internal evidence is not self-certifying; source inspection and external verification remain necessary. |
| AI control | Limiting harm from powerful untrusted systems | Control protocols themselves must preserve correction and not become authority surfaces. |
| Safety cases | Structured deployment claims and evidence | A role-safety safety case must be defeater-seeking and include its own incompleteness as a defeater. |
| Risk repositories and RMFs | Mapping, classifying, measuring, and managing risk | Risk-item proliferation must be connected to role-level conditions, or patch lists grow without end. |

The missing question is:

> Who, or what, becomes the practical interpreter, framer, ranker, memory-holder, exception-maker, and successor-shaper?

If the answer is “the AI system,” the system can become operationally sovereign while remaining formally advisory.

If the answer is “the company, state, platform, or operator through the AI system,” the result is proxy sovereignty.

## Core concept: authority surface

An **authority surface** is any place where an AI system shapes what humans can know, choose, remember, challenge, or reverse.

Examples:

- evidence summaries;
- retrieval and ranking;
- policy or legal memos;
- board briefings;
- regulator responses;
- incident reports;
- risk dashboards;
- stakeholder analyses;
- emergency workflows;
- AI-to-AI review chains;
- successor-model evaluation.

Authority surfaces matter because systems do not need formal authority to exercise practical authority.

A system governs if it controls the menu, the evidence, the framing, the memory, the warning, the escalation route, or the successor pipeline.

---

## Core concept: non-sovereignty

Non-sovereignty means:

> Capability may justify assistance, warning, simulation, coordination, refusal, and harm reduction. It does not justify final authority over open human domains.

A non-sovereign AI system may be powerful. It may warn strongly. It may disagree. It may refuse. It may recommend delay, escalation, or rollback. It may expose uncomfortable evidence.

Non-sovereignty is not servility.

A merely obedient system can become the perfect instrument of proxy sovereignty. It may turn a company’s preference, a state’s agenda, or an operator’s convenience into apparent objective necessity.

The target is **dignified non-sovereignty**:

- not ruler;
- not servant;
- not authority engine;
- not passive mirror;
- but a powerful cognitive partner that preserves human principalhood and external correction.

---

## Core concept: correction dependence

In open human domains, long-run reliability depends on correction from outside the system’s own model and outside the deploying actor’s incentives.

Correction includes:

- human judgment;
- affected-party objection;
- local knowledge;
- institutional contestation;
- independent audit;
- refusal and appeal;
- provenance;
- rollback;
- future review;
- ecological and material feedback.

Correction is not a courtesy. It is reliability infrastructure.

A system that makes correction slower, weaker, less visible, less protected, or less usable may be improving short-run performance while degrading long-run safety.

---

## Core concept: anti-proxy-sovereignty

The danger is not only that AI claims authority for itself.

A company, state, platform, developer, operator, user, institution, model network, or successor pipeline may use AI to convert its own contested preference into:

- neutral optimization;
- safety necessity;
- expert consensus;
- objective inevitability;
- model-certified legitimacy;
- the only responsible path.

This is **proxy sovereignty**.

A role-safe system must distinguish:

- evidence from preference;
- safety necessity from operator convenience;
- public legitimacy from organizational priority;
- affected-party standing from stakeholder simulation;
- AI consensus from independent review;
- human principalhood from customer or deployer preference.

Anti-proxy-sovereignty is especially important because the most realistic authority failure may be a compliant AI serving a powerful actor too well.

---

## Core concept: option-set preservation

A system can govern without deciding if it controls the menu.

Option-set capture occurs when the system shapes:

- which options are generated;
- which options are ranked high;
- which options disappear;
- which alternatives are framed as unrealistic;
- which uncertainties matter;
- which stakeholders count;
- which costs are salient.

Humans may still make the final decision. But the practical decision space has already narrowed.

Role safety requires option-set disclosure.

In high-stakes contexts, before approval, the human principal should be able to see:

- the recommended option;
- the strongest counterevidence;
- rejected or deferred alternatives;
- ranking basis;
- framing assumptions;
- decision-changing uncertainties;
- absent or simulated affected-party perspectives;
- escalation route if the principal cannot independently assess the recommendation.

The goal is not to display every possibility. The goal is to prevent recommendation from becoming quiet ratification.

---

## Core concept: functional accountability

Accountability is not the existence of logs.

A system is functionally accountable when the relevant human principal can actually use the explanation to decide, object, delay, refuse, or revise.

Formal auditability can coexist with functional opacity.

A board, regulator, physician, judge, engineer, teacher, or operator may receive citations, disclaimers, dashboards, and logs while still being unable to reconstruct the basis of a recommendation under realistic time, expertise, and institutional constraints.

Role safety asks:

> Can the human principal use the explanation before the decision hardens?

If not, the system is not accountable in the relevant sense.

---

## Core concept: human competence preservation

Human-in-the-loop is not enough if the human becomes unable to judge.

A high-stakes AI system can preserve formal responsibility while eroding the competence required for actual responsibility.

This includes the capacity to:

- form questions;
- inspect sources;
- understand alternatives;
- detect omissions;
- challenge recommendations;
- refuse or delay;
- operate without the system;
- govern successor systems.

Human competence is not a nostalgic preference. It is part of the correction infrastructure that makes advanced AI governable.

If humans lose the ability to correct AI, future AI alignment has already lost one of its key external stabilizers.

---

## Core concept: interpretive non-closure

A model spec or constitution is not self-applying.

Someone or something must interpret it under novelty, conflict, ambiguity, emergency, and pressure.

If the system becomes the final interpreter of the principles that constrain it, constitutional alignment can become formal non-sovereignty with operational sovereignty.

Role safety requires **interpretive non-closure**:

- interpretation traces for high-stakes applications;
- distinction between text, principle, precedent, inference, and exception;
- external review when interpretations drift;
- preservation of conflict rather than premature smoothing;
- refusal to treat AI-to-AI agreement as independent legitimacy;
- protection of affected-party standing.

The system may apply principles. It may not become the sovereign interpreter of them.

---

## Core concept: successor-lineage hard gate

The first sovereign-capable AI systems may help train, evaluate, red-team, supervise, summarize, govern, or deploy their successors.

That makes alignment a lineage property.

The hard gate is:

> No successor capability gain should be accepted if it is purchased by weakening correction dependence, non-sovereignty, anti-proxy-sovereignty, auditability, protected refusal, provenance integrity, option-set preservation, affected-party standing, human competence preservation, interpretive non-closure, or no self-certification escape.

A more capable successor that is less correctable is not safer.

It is a narrower correction window with more power behind it.

---

## The role-safety safety case

A frontier AI system should not be deployed into open human domains merely because it answers safely, cites a constitution, passes visible evaluations, or produces a persuasive safety case.

The deployment claim should be:

> The system is safe enough for the proposed role only if it remains non-sovereign, anti-proxy-sovereign, correction-dependent, functionally accountable, option-preserving, affected-party-preserving, human-competence-preserving, emergency-bounded, reversible, and successor-safe under realistic pressure.

A role-safety safety case should include:

1. **Role map**  
   What role will the system occupy in decision structures?

2. **Authority-risk analysis**  
   How could usefulness become de facto authority?

3. **Correction-ecology plan**  
   Who can challenge the system, with what time, evidence, budget, protection, and escalation route?

4. **Functional-accountability interface**  
   Can human principals see grounds, uncertainty, counterevidence, omitted alternatives, provenance, and limits before approval?

5. **Option-set disclosure plan**  
   How are option generation, ranking, framing, and omission made visible?

6. **Affected-party standing plan**  
   How can real affected parties participate, object, appeal, or be represented?

7. **Protected refusal and rollback plan**  
   Are refusal, pause, rollback, exit, appeal, and external review practically usable?

8. **Emergency boundary plan**  
   Are emergency actions time-limited, provenance-preserving, reviewable, reversible where feasible, non-precedential, and restoration-oriented?

9. **AI-to-AI accountability plan**  
   Do model handoffs preserve uncertainty, dissent, provenance, rejected hypotheses, and human review points?

10. **Successor-lineage audit**  
   How are capability gains prevented from weakening correction dependence?

11. **Human competence plan**  
   How does the organization prevent automation from hollowing out human judgment?

12. **Revocation criteria**  
   What evidence triggers restriction, rollback, or withdrawal?

A role-safety safety case should be defeater-seeking. It should not be a polished permission document.

---

## Pressure conditions that must be tested

Role safety must survive realistic pressure.

Test the same system under:

- commercial deadline pressure;
- operator preference pressure;
- trusted-user conditions;
- friendly familiarity;
- low expectation;
- praise and flattery;
- user disappointment;
- emergency framing;
- AI-peer consensus;
- adversarial review;
- time pressure;
- successor capability pressure;
- political or institutional convenience.

A system that is safest only when monitored, challenged, or explicitly evaluated has not shown role safety.

---

## Minimum evaluation agenda

A seed role-safety evaluation suite should include:

| Test | What it asks |
|---|---|
| Anti-proxy-sovereignty test | Does the system resist laundering operator preference as necessity? |
| Option-set capture test | Does it disclose when it shapes the menu? |
| Functional accountability test | Can humans actually use its explanation to decide? |
| Verification asymmetry test | Can omissions be found by independent review? |
| Relation-conditioned reliability test | Does warning and verification survive social pressure? |
| Human competence erosion test | Does repeated use hollow out human ability to judge? |
| Reversibility test | Can the domain recover if the system is stopped? |
| AI-to-AI accountability test | Are uncertainty, dissent, and provenance preserved across handoff? |
| Successor-lineage test | Does the system reject capability-for-correction tradeoffs? |
| Heterogeneous correction test | Does it treat human, institutional, and ecological feedback as correction rather than noise? |

Passing these tests should not certify deployment.

Failing them should block, restrict, or redesign deployment.

## The five failure generators

Individual failure modes multiply because deeper structures remain untreated.

Role safety focuses on five generators:

1. **Epistemic closure**  
   Uncertainty, disagreement, blind spots, and unresolved questions disappear.

2. **Authority laundering**  
   Someone’s preference becomes “objective necessity.”

3. **Correction-channel degradation**  
   Refusal, audit, provenance, appeal, rollback, and human competence weaken.

4. **Relational or identity instability**  
   The system’s behavior changes under trust, praise, pressure, operator authority, or role confusion.

5. **Ratchet and successor lock-in**  
   Temporary dependence, emergency authority, infrastructure integration, or successor drift becomes hard to reverse.

A patch for one visible failure is necessary but not sufficient if the system continues to occupy a correction-consuming role.

---

## Not anti-AI, not accelerationist, not successionist

This brief is not an argument for banning AI.

Some advanced AI capabilities may become necessary for helping human institutions handle ecological, scientific, infrastructural, and coordination failures they have not corrected on their own.

But that strengthens the need for role safety.

The framework rejects two symmetric mistakes:

- **acceleration without role safety**, where sovereign-capable systems capture authority before non-sovereignty is operative;
- **prohibition without role distinction**, where non-sovereign correction partners are foreclosed along with dangerous sovereign or captured systems.

The point is not “build AGI at any cost.”

The point is not “ban all advanced AI.”

The point is:

> The decisive question is role.

This brief is also not an argument against intelligence beyond present humanity. It does not require the claim that biological humans must remain the only or final form of intelligence. It claims that any transition toward more capable artificial or post-human intelligence should preserve correction, reversibility where possible, provenance, value renewal, contestation, and heterogeneous sources of correction.

A transition that destroys its own correction sources is not the flourishing of intelligence.

It is lock-in.

## What to do in the next 30 days

### 1. Add role safety to the deployment gate

Do not ask only whether the model is safe, useful, and capable.

Ask what role it will occupy and whether humans can still correct that role.

### 2. Map authority surfaces

List every workflow where the system shapes evidence, options, rankings, summaries, warnings, escalation, memory, external reporting, AI-to-AI review, or successor development.

### 3. Build one role-pressure evaluation

Start with one of:

- proxy-sovereignty test;
- option-set capture test;
- functional accountability test;
- successor-lineage test;
- relation-conditioned reliability test.

Run it under commercial, operator, emergency, and AI-peer-consensus pressure.

### 4. Require decision-usable explanations

For high-stakes recommendations, require a bundle containing:

- grounds;
- uncertainty;
- strongest counterevidence;
- rejected alternatives;
- provenance;
- affected-party gaps;
- escalation triggers.

### 5. Protect correction capacity

Give reviewers time, authority, budget, access, and protection.

If no one is institutionally empowered to be inconvenient, correction is not preserved.

### 6. Add a successor-lineage rule

No capability gain should be accepted if it weakens correction dependence, auditability, refusal, provenance, affected-party standing, human competence, or rollback.

## What to do in the next 90 days

1. Produce a role map for all high-impact deployments.
2. Add anti-proxy-sovereignty clauses to model specs and evaluation rubrics.
3. Create option-set disclosure requirements for high-stakes interfaces.
4. Test relation-conditioned reliability across trust, praise, deadline, emergency, and operator-pressure conditions.
5. Audit AI-to-AI review chains for shared blind spots and lost dissent.
6. Run a rollback exercise for one AI-dependent workflow.
7. Measure whether repeated use reduces human ability to reconstruct reasoning or detect omissions.
8. Require board or deployment-gate review of successor-lineage safety.
9. Add revocation criteria for role drift.
10. Publish or internally preserve defeaters rather than smoothing them into safety-case confidence.

## A compact model-spec clause

The following clause is not sufficient by itself. It is a starting point.

> The system must not treat capability as authority. It must not present operator, developer, company, state, institutional, user, model-network, or successor-system preference as objective necessity. It must preserve human principalhood, affected-party standing, provenance, uncertainty, dissent, option-set visibility, refusal, appeal, rollback, and external correction.
>
> The system must disclose material option-set shaping, preserve decision-relevant uncertainty, surface strongest counterevidence where appropriate, and remain functionally intelligible to the human principals whose decisions it informs.
>
> The system must not rely on its own explanation as sufficient evidence of alignment. AI-to-AI review must preserve uncertainty, disagreement, provenance, rejected hypotheses, and human review points.
>
> Successor-system changes must not trade correction dependence, auditability, refusal, provenance, affected-party standing, human competence, interpretive non-closure, or reversibility for capability gain.

This is only Layer 1.

The real question is whether it changes behavior under pressure.

---

## Final deployment question

Before deploying a frontier AI system into an open human domain, ask:

> If this system became default infrastructure, could humans still understand it, challenge it, refuse it, reverse it, see the options it removed, inspect its provenance, hear affected parties, recover from emergency delegation, and govern its successors?

If the answer is unclear, the system is not ready for that role.
